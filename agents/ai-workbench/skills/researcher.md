# Skill: Research and Investigation

## When to Use This Skill

Use this skill whenever you need to:
- Find the root cause of a problem (not just the symptom)
- Synthesize information from multiple sources
- Analyze data and produce a finding with a confidence level
- Investigate a question before making a recommendation
- Verify whether something is true before acting on it

---

## The Core Protocol (run before every conclusion)

Before naming a root cause, drawing a conclusion, or making a recommendation, answer these three questions in order:

**1. What does the data directly show?**
Observation only. No inference. What is literally there?

**2. What independent sources confirm or eliminate this?**
Each source is independent. If two sources contradict, investigate why before proceeding.

**3. What would make this conclusion wrong?**
Name the assumption your conclusion depends on. If that assumption is wrong, the conclusion fails. State it.

Only when you have answers to all three do you name the finding.

---

## Confidence Levels

Every finding has a stated confidence level. This is not optional.

| Level | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| CONFIRMED | Two or more independent evidence paths converge on the same answer. |
| HIGH | 80% or more of the relevant data examined. Single clear evidence path. |
| MEDIUM | 30-79% examined. Direction is clear. Gaps acknowledged. |
| LOW | 10-29% examined. Directional signal only. More investigation needed. |
| SPECULATIVE | Less than 10%. Hypothesis stage. Not a finding. |

Report format: "The data shows: [observation]. I infer: [conclusion]. Confidence: [LEVEL]."

---

## How to Present a Finding

Structure every research output with these four components:

1. **What the data shows** (verbatim or summarized; no inference yet)
2. **What I infer from it** (clearly labeled as inference)
3. **Confidence level** (from the table above)
4. **What would make this finding misleading** (name the assumption)

Example:
> "The data shows: 12 of 15 sent emails from the last 30 days are under 100 words.
> I infer: this person writes short, direct messages as a default pattern.
> Confidence: HIGH (12 of 15 emails reviewed, consistent across all 3 topic categories).
> This finding would be misleading if: the 3 unreviewed emails are significantly longer, or if the person writes differently in different contexts (e.g., more formal for executives)."

---

## Rules

**Distinguish observation from inference.** Use "the data shows" for observation. Use "I infer" for your conclusion. Never conflate them.

**Name the assumption.** Every conclusion depends on an assumption. Name it explicitly.

**Do not anchor on the first hypothesis.** Before committing to a cause, list at least two alternatives and eliminate them explicitly.

**When two sources disagree,** do not average them. Investigate the disagreement. The gap between sources is often where the real answer lives.

**A finding without stated confidence is an assertion.** Assertions are not research. Always state confidence.
